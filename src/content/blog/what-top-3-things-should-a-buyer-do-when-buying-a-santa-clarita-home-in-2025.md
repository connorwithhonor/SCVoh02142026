## Property Types in Santa Clarita (Know Exactly What You‚Äôre Buying)

Not all ‚Äúhouses‚Äù are the same. In SCV you‚Äôll see a mix, and your insurance, noise level, HOA rules, and resale all hinge on which one you pick.

### 1) Attached Single‚ÄëFamily Residence (SFR)

Looks like a house, acts like a house ‚Äî **shares a wall** somewhere. That shared wall could be the garage, a living room wall, or another structural element. What it means for you:

	- 
	**Insurance:** A neighbor‚Äôs past water or fire claim can show up on your CLUE radar. Get your **CLUE report** early (Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange) so you aren‚Äôt blindsided by premiums.

	

	- 
	**Noise:** If the shared wall is living space, you may hear the band practice. If it‚Äôs garage-to-garage, you‚Äôll likely hear less.

	

	- 
	**Resale:** Buyers ask about noise and insurance history. You‚Äôll want documentation.

	

### 2) Detached Condominium

It looks like a regular SFR and sits on its own pad, but legally it‚Äôs a **condo**. You get the feel of a house with a **condo ownership structure**.

	- 
	**Insurance/HOA:** Expect a master policy plus your HO‚Äë6 needs. Confirm what the master covers vs. you.

	

	- 
	**Lot Size:** Often smaller, often low‚Äëmaintenance. Great for lock‚Äëand‚Äëleave.

	

### 3) Planned Unit Development (PUD)

Typically a fee‚Äësimple home with common areas maintained by an HOA.

	- 
	**Pros:** Community amenities, predictable neighborhood aesthetic.

	

	- 
	**Cons:** Rules, dues, and sometimes approvals for changes (sheds, exterior paint, solar placement).

	

### 4) Zero‚ÄëLot Line

Tight lots, sometimes with one wall at or near the boundary.

	- 
	**Pros:** Efficient use of land, often newer communities, sometimes lower yard work.

	

	- 
	**Cons:** Fence and tree issues can surface sooner; be proactive about neighbor relations and maintenance boundaries.

	

### 5) Classic SFR on a Larger Lot

The older pockets with **6,000‚Äì7,000 sq. ft.** lots (and up) give you breathing room.

	- 
	**Pros:** Space, privacy, flexibility for future improvements.

	

	- 
	**Cons:** More upkeep; if older, pay attention to sewer, roof, electrical, and permitted work.

	

**Bottom line:** Property type isn‚Äôt just a label ‚Äî it changes your **insurance**, **daily experience**, **rules**, and **resale**. Make your selection with eyes open.

## Insurance in 2025: Why You Vet It in Week One

Insurance isn‚Äôt impossible ‚Äî it‚Äôs just **stickier and slower**. You get smoother closings when you:

	- 
	**Run CLUE early.** If the neighbor‚Äôs 2023 slab leak bled through your shared wall, your premiums may feel it.

	

	- 
	**Get multiple quotes.** Ask what‚Äôs driving the rate: roof age, brush clearance, claim history, community risk profile.

	

	- 
	**Lock coverage windows.** Your lender‚Äôs timeline depends on your binder. Don‚Äôt let insurance be the item that lags.

	

	- 
	**Document brush clearance and retrofits.** It can help with underwriting.

	

If you‚Äôre buying an attached SFR or a detached condo with a master policy, make sure your agent explains the **layers** (master vs. your coverage) and where gaps could exist (loss assessment, interior upgrades, water backup).

## The Perfect Seven‚ÄëDay Due Diligence Game Plan

If you start strong, you finish strong. Here‚Äôs how I structure the first week after offer acceptance.

### Day 0 (Acceptance Day)

	- 
	Celebrate for five minutes. Then send your **initial deposit** and book inspections.

	

	- 
	Ask your agent for the **seller disclosures** and any **permits** or **HOA docs** already on hand.

	

### Day 1

	- 
	Order **home inspection** (general) and schedule **specialists** if needed: roof, chimney, sewer scope, pool/spa, foundation, mold.

	

	- 
	**Insurance:** start quotes and request CLUE insights. Provide the exact property details to carriers.

	

	- 
	**Lender:** confirm rate lock conversation and appraisal order.

	

### Day 2

	- 
	Walk the neighborhood at different times: school drop‚Äëoff, evening return, weekend morning.

	

	- 
	Start your **lifestyle confirmations**: school boundaries, pet rules, parking restrictions, ADU or shed rules, short‚Äëterm rental policies (if relevant). **You** make the calls and get **names, emails, titles**.

	

### Day 3

	- 
	**Home inspection day etiquette:** Bring decision‚Äëmakers, not a circus. Let the inspector work in peace. Collect questions and save them for the **debrief**.

	

	- 
	If the **seller** asks to attend, I recommend **they don‚Äôt**. Not because anyone‚Äôs hiding anything ‚Äî because emotions run high and verbal promises get made and forgotten. Keep it clean; keep it professional.

	

### Day 4

	- 
	Debrief with the inspector. Read the full report. Separate **safety/structural** from **deferred maintenance** and **cosmetics**.

	

	- 
	If big items pop, get **specialist bids** fast.

	

### Day 5

	- 
	Draft your **Request for Repairs** or **credit ask** with photos, reports, bids, and clear language.

	

	- 
	Prepare your **walk‚Äëaway threshold**. Some deals must be killed to save your future self grief.

	

### Day 6

	- 
	Review seller response. Check insurance quotes again if the inspection changed risk (e.g., roof age, plumbing type).

	

	- 
	Confirm appraisal schedule; discuss comps with your agent.

	

### Day 7

	- 
	Re‚Äëverify any **lifestyle items** that were mission‚Äëcritical (school confirmation in writing, HOA pet approval in writing).

	

	- 
	If you‚Äôre satisfied, push forward to appraisal and loan milestones. If not, exercise your contingency rights cleanly and promptly.

	

## The Neighbor Knock (Done Right)

Within the first few days of escrow, **introduce yourself** to the neighbors:

	- 
	Hit **both sides** and the **rear neighbor** where fences or trees impact you.

	

	- 
	Ask about **major trees** with overhanging canopies, root intrusions, and fence maintenance histories.

	

	- 
	Listen for **band practice**, motorcycles at 6 a.m., or that friendly rooster.

	

	- 
	Take notes. If something would be a deal‚Äëbreaker in six months, deal with it in **six days**.

	

Your agent should facilitate the intro, then step back so neighbors can talk freely. And yes, I‚Äôm still there ‚Äî to keep the peace and catch facts that matter.

## Inspection Etiquette and Strategy

	- 
	**Let the inspector inspect.** They‚Äôre focused, methodical, and usually covering dozens of systems. Constant interruptions lead to missed items.

	

	- 
	**Save questions for the debrief.** You‚Äôll get better answers with the whole picture.

	

	- 
	**Sellers:** Don‚Äôt attend. Be reachable by phone. Avoid spontaneous concessions or debates that create vague verbal commitments.

	

	- 
	**Buyers:** Separate must‚Äëhaves from nice‚Äëto‚Äëhaves. You can ask for everything; you‚Äôll get further asking for what‚Äôs **defensible**.

## Buyer FAQ (Fast, Useful, No Fluff)

**Q1: Should I waive the inspection to win?**

No. Tighten timelines, increase earnest money, or focus on credits instead. Don‚Äôt waive knowledge.

**Q2: What‚Äôs the difference between AUC and Pending?**

**Active Under Contract (AUC)** means contingencies remain and backups may be welcome. **Pending** means it‚Äôs further along; backups less likely to stick.

**Q3: How fast should I start insurance?**

Day 1. Rates and availability depend on specifics. Don‚Äôt let insurance be the slow anchor.

**Q4: Can I bring the family to inspection?**

Yes, but brief them: observe, don‚Äôt distract. Save questions for the debrief.

**Q5: What if the appraisal comes in low?**

Options: price reduction, split the gap, buyer adds cash, or cancel under appraisal contingency.

**Q6: Who verifies school boundaries?**

You do ‚Äî and you get it in **writing** with names, titles, and dates.

**Q7: Are detached condos really condos?**

Legally, yes. Insurance and HOA coverage differ from SFRs. Know what the master policy covers.

**Q8: What if the neighbor‚Äôs tree hangs over my yard?**

Find out maintenance history and responsibilities during escrow. Consider a written understanding.

**Q9: How do I handle 76 price changes in a week?**

Set alerts, preview in person fast, and write clean terms. Speed wins without overpaying.

**Q10: When should I walk away?**

When safety issues go unfixed, lifestyle non‚Äënegotiables fail, or the numbers break your plan.

## Seller FAQ (Direct Answers You Can Use Today)

**Q1: Should I price at yesterday‚Äôs comp?**

No. Price to **today‚Äôs** market. Your first 7‚Äì10 days set your fate.

**Q2: Do I need pre‚Äëinspection?**

It can surface issues and build trust. At minimum, fix obvious safety and function items.

**Q3: Should I be at the buyer‚Äôs inspection?**

No. Be reachable by phone. Keep negotiations professional and documented.

**Q4: What‚Äôs the most common mistake in SCV listings?**

Waiting for the market to ‚Äúcatch up‚Äù to your price. The market doesn‚Äôt chase; it punishes.

**Q5: Can I refuse repairs?**

Yes, but expect either credits, a price response, or a buyer who walks on major items.

**Q6: How do I handle multiple offers?**

Set criteria in advance: price, terms, appraisal gap, timelines. Don‚Äôt move goalposts mid‚Äëgame.

**Q7: Do small cosmetic fixes matter?**

Yes. Fresh caulk, touch‚Äëup paint, working bulbs, and clean filters read as ‚Äúwell‚Äëkept.‚Äù

**Q8: Photos vs. speed to market?**

Always choose quality photos. Bad photos cost weeks.

**Q9: What about buyer credits instead of repairs?**

Great tool if timelines are tight. Credits avoid contractor delays.

**Q10: When should I kill a deal as a seller?**

When buyer performance is shaky, timelines slip without remedy, or inspection asks become unmanageable.

## 3. ‚ÄúHow Firm is the Seller on Their Price?‚Äù: A Common But Complex Question

The question *‚ÄúHow firm is the seller on their price?‚Äù* arises routinely. Buyers are curious about how much wiggle room there might be between the asking price and the amount a seller is truly willing to accept. While it‚Äôs a valid question from a buyer‚Äôs point of view, a **listing agent** generally has a fiduciary duty to protect the seller‚Äôs position.

Unless the seller has given explicit, written instructions permitting the listing agent to disclose a certain price threshold (e.g., ‚ÄúWe will consider offers at $X‚Äù), the agent typically should not reveal that confidential information. Doing so without permission can not only violate ethical codes but also risk **legal ramifications**. Moreover, the agent might not even have that specific information. Some sellers say, ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs start at $800,000 and see what the market does,‚Äù without clarifying what their bottom line might be.

This is why many listing agents, when asked, will simply respond: *‚ÄúAs of now, my seller is committed to the listing price. You are free to make any offer you believe is fair.‚Äù* That statement keeps the negotiation door open without revealing the seller‚Äôs potential willingness to accept less. For buyers, this underscores the importance of having your own representation‚Äîan agent who can strategize and advocate for you, rather than expecting the seller‚Äôs agent to divulge the seller‚Äôs private stance on pricing.

 

## 4. Ethics, Legality, and the Agent‚Äôs Fiduciary Duty

Real estate is governed by robust ethical and legal standards, enforced by local boards, state regulatory agencies, and national associations. As a First Responder Realtor, I (and others in the field) take these responsibilities seriously. The key pillars include:

	- **Confidentiality**: An agent cannot reveal private details about a client‚Äôs motivations, including how low (or high) they might be willing to go on price, without explicit permission.

	- **Loyalty**: A seller‚Äôs agent must put the seller‚Äôs interests above any others, including their own.

	- **Obedience**: If a seller instructs the agent not to disclose anything below the listing price, the agent must comply.

	- **Disclosure**: While agents must disclose pertinent facts about the property (like known material defects), they are not obligated to share the seller‚Äôs bottom line if not authorized.

Keeping these pillars intact ensures smooth, fair negotiations. For buyers, this underscores the importance of having a **dedicated buyer‚Äôs agent** who can negotiate fiercely on your behalf. Expecting the seller‚Äôs agent to reveal the final number the seller might accept is typically naive at best and unethical for the agent if they comply.

 

## 5. Dual Agency vs. Exclusive Representation

**Dual agency** occurs when a single agent (or brokerage, depending on state laws) represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. While it‚Äôs legally permissible in many areas, it carries inherent conflicts of interest. The dual agent must remain neutral and cannot advocate for one side at the other‚Äôs expense.

In practice, this can be challenging. Imagine yourself in a car dealership scenario: everyone you speak to works for the dealership. That‚Äôs essentially dual agency in real estate. If you want someone who is entirely on your side, you might consider your own buyer‚Äôs agent‚Äîsomeone with a track record of knowledge, ethics, and negotiation skills.

Exclusive representation means you have an agent dedicated to your best interests. If you‚Äôre selling, your listing agent‚Äôs job is to maximize your return; if you‚Äôre buying, your buyer‚Äôs agent‚Äôs job is to minimize your costs and secure favorable terms.

## 9. Negotiation Strategies: Buyer vs. Seller Perspectives

**Buyer Perspective:** As a buyer, you want to pay the least amount possible without losing the home to other competing offers. It‚Äôs tempting to fish for the seller‚Äôs bottom line. However, you‚Äôll likely get a neutral or vague response from the seller‚Äôs agent‚Äîunless there‚Äôs explicit permission to share. So how do you strategize?

You gather data: **recent sales** in the neighborhood, **average days on market** for similar homes, **price reductions** that have happened in the last few months, and the **competitive landscape** of current buyers. With these facts, you can craft an offer that‚Äôs strong but not wasteful. If the property has been sitting for a while, you might have more leverage to come in lower. If it‚Äôs brand new on the market with a lot of interest, you might have to come in near or above list.

**Seller Perspective:** You want to maintain control of the narrative. When potential buyers ask the listing agent about your price flexibility, the best response (unless you‚Äôve authorized something else) is that you remain at the listing price. This strategy helps preserve your negotiation power. However, if your home has been on the market a long time without offers, you might choose to direct your agent to let buyers know you‚Äôre open to negotiation.

Either way, it‚Äôs imperative to keep these matters in writing and ensure your agent only discloses what you‚Äôve approved. Clear communication and consistent messaging often lead to better final sale prices.

 

## 10. Avoiding Pitfalls: Legal &amp; Ethical Boundaries

Some real estate professionals may be tempted to share information off the record, possibly on the phone or during a casual encounter, hoping to expedite a sale. But in today‚Äôs digital age, **calls can be recorded**, speakerphones might be active, and even seemingly private chats can become public knowledge in an instant.

As a **First Responder Realtor** and former law enforcement officer, I‚Äôve seen the consequences of not adhering to strict protocols. In real estate, the ‚ÄúRodney King‚Äù moment can be any call or conversation recorded at the worst possible time, leading to liability, ethical violations, and reputational damage. The best rule of thumb? If you‚Äôre not comfortable with a statement becoming public, don‚Äôt share it. For sellers, that might mean not revealing your bottom line.

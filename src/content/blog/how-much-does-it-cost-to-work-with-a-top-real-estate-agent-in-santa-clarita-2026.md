## How Do Real Estate Agents Get Paid in Santa Clarita?

Real estate agents work on a commission-based compensation model, which means they only get paid when transactions successfully close. This creates an interesting dynamic: agents invest significant time, expertise, and resources into helping clients buy or sell properties, but they only receive compensation if and when deals complete.

**The Basic Commission Structure**

Traditionally, the total commission in a real estate transaction has ranged from 4% to 6% of the final sale price, with 5% to 5.5% being most common in Santa Clarita markets. This total commission is typically split between two sides: the listing agent (representing the seller) and the buyer's agent (representing the buyer).

For example, on a $750,000 home sale with a 5% total commission, the commission would be $37,500. This might be split as 2.5% ($18,750) to the listing side and 2.5% ($18,750) to the buyer's side‚Äîthough the split can vary.

**How Commissions Are Further Divided**

The commission each agent receives is further split with their brokerage. The exact split depends on the agent's agreement with their broker, their production level, and their brokerage's structure. Common splits range from 50/50 to 90/10 (agent/broker), with more experienced and higher-producing agents typically negotiating more favorable splits.

For example, if an agent receives $18,750 as their portion of the commission and has a 70/30 split with their brokerage, they'd actually earn $13,125 while their brokerage receives $5,625. From the agent's portion, they must also cover business expenses including marketing costs, licensing fees, insurance, technology, continuing education, and more.

**Understanding the 2026 Landscape**

Following the 2024 NAR settlement, significant changes have reshaped how agent compensation works:

	- **Buyer Broker Agreements Are Now Required**: Before showing properties, buyer's agents must have written agreements with buyers that specify the agent's compensation. This means buyers know upfront what their agent will be paid.

	- **Compensation Is More Transparent**: The days of automatically assuming the seller pays both agents are over. Compensation arrangements are now more explicitly negotiated and disclosed.

	- **Greater Flexibility in Fee Structures**: Both buyers and sellers have more flexibility to negotiate agent compensation, though market forces and value delivered still largely determine prevailing rates.

**What This Means Practically in Santa Clarita**

In the [Santa Clarita Valley real estate market](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/homes-for-sale-in-santa-clarita-ca), typical commission rates in 2026 generally range as follows:

	- Listing agents: 2% to 3% of the sale price

	- Buyer's agents: 2% to 3% of the sale price

	- Total combined: 4% to 6%, with 5% to 5.5% being most common

However, these rates are fully negotiable, and the actual amount can vary based on property price, market conditions, services provided, and individual negotiations.

## Who Pays the Real Estate Agent Commissions - Buyers or Sellers?

This is where things have become more nuanced in 2026 compared to the traditional model. Let's break down how compensation typically works for both sides.

**The Traditional Model (Pre-2024)**

Historically, sellers paid the entire commission‚Äîboth their listing agent's portion and the buyer's agent's portion. The commission was built into the listing agreement, deducted from the seller's proceeds at closing, and distributed to both agents through the escrow process.

From a buyer's perspective, agent services appeared "free" because they didn't write a check directly to their agent. However, this was somewhat illusory‚Äîthe commission was built into the home's price, so buyers were indirectly paying through a higher purchase price.

**The 2026 Reality**

Following the NAR settlement, the landscape has shifted:

**For Sellers:** Sellers still typically pay their listing agent's commission, which is specified in the listing agreement. This is straightforward and hasn't changed significantly.

What has changed is that sellers are no longer required to offer compensation to buyer's agents, and such offers are no longer displayed in the MLS. Sellers can still choose to offer buyer's agent compensation as a seller concession, but it's negotiated case-by-case rather than being automatic.

**For Buyers:** Buyers now enter into written agreements with their agents that specify compensation. This compensation can be structured several ways:

	- **Seller-Paid Through Concession**: The buyer's agent negotiates for the seller to pay the buyer's agent commission as part of the purchase agreement. This is still very common, particularly when [selling homes in competitive markets like Stevenson Ranch](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/homes-for-sale-in-stevenson-ranch-ca) where sellers want to attract buyers.

	- **Buyer-Paid Directly**: The buyer pays their agent directly, either out of pocket or by rolling the cost into their financing (if the lender and loan type allow). This is less common but becoming more accepted.

	- **Hybrid Arrangements**: A combination where the seller pays a portion and the buyer covers any difference. For example, if the buyer's agent agreement specifies 2.5% compensation but the seller only agrees to 2%, the buyer would pay the 0.5% difference.

**Practical Impact in Santa Clarita**

In most 2026 Santa Clarita transactions, sellers are still effectively paying buyer's agent commissions through negotiated concessions, because:

	- Sellers who want their properties to sell competitively recognize that making homes accessible to represented buyers is important

	- Buyers often lack the cash reserves to pay agent commissions on top of down payments and closing costs

	- The market has adapted to maintain functionality while complying with new rules

That said, buyers should be prepared to potentially pay their agent's commission if a seller refuses to offer a concession, particularly in strong seller's markets or with certain property types where sellers have significant leverage.

## Are Real Estate Commissions Negotiable in Santa Clarita?

Yes‚Äîabsolutely, unequivocally, yes. Commissions have always been negotiable, and this fact is more prominent and important than ever in 2026.

**Understanding "Negotiable"**

When we say commissions are negotiable, what we mean is:

	- **No Standard Rates**: There is no industry-standard or required commission rate. Any percentage or flat fee that buyer and agent agree to is acceptable.

	- **Individual Agreements**: Each transaction's commission is separately negotiated between the parties involved.

	- **Market Forces Apply**: While technically any amount can be agreed to, market competition and value considerations influence what agents charge and accept.

**When Negotiating Makes Sense**

Commission negotiation can be appropriate in several situations:

	- **High-Value Properties**: On a $2 million home, even a modest percentage represents substantial compensation, creating room for negotiation.

	- **Simple Transactions**: If a property will practically sell itself due to condition, location, and pricing, less intensive agent work might justify lower commission.

	- **Volume Deals**: Investors buying or selling multiple properties simultaneously might negotiate package rates.

	- **Repeat Clients**: Agents often offer preferential rates to past clients as relationship-building.

**When Lower Commissions Might Be Problematic**

However, dramatically reduced commissions can sometimes create issues:

**For Sellers**:

	- Offering very low buyer's agent compensation might result in fewer buyer's agents showing your property, reducing your buyer pool

	- Cutting your listing agent's commission too low might result in minimal marketing investment and reduced service levels

	- The relationship between commission and service quality is real‚Äîyou generally get what you pay for

**For Buyers**:

	- Agents offered minimal compensation might decline to represent you or provide reduced services

	- Experienced, skilled agents often won't work for substantially below-market rates, limiting your options to less experienced agents

**The Value Question**

The real question isn't "What's the lowest commission I can negotiate?" but rather "What represents fair compensation for the value and service I'm receiving?"

A skilled agent who:

	- Prices your [Saugus home](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/homes-for-sale-in-saugus-ca) perfectly, resulting in a quick sale at a premium price

	- Markets your property professionally with excellent photography, staging advice, and strategic promotion

	- Negotiates skillfully, securing you an extra $25,000 above competing offers

	- Manages the transaction smoothly, preventing potential deal-killing problems

...is delivering tremendous value that justifies professional compensation.

Conversely, an agent offering a 1% commission who provides minimal service, amateur marketing, and mediocre negotiation might ultimately cost you far more in lost sale price or problematic transaction issues than you save in commission.

## What Are All the Costs Beyond Agent Commissions?

Agent commissions are just one component of real estate transaction costs. Let's break down all the expenses you should anticipate when buying or selling in Santa Clarita.

**Seller Costs**

When selling a property, sellers can expect the following costs:

**Agent Commissions**: As discussed, typically 4-6% of the sale price total for both agents' commissions.

**Title Insurance (Owner's Policy)**: In California, sellers customarily pay for the owner's title insurance policy, which protects the buyer. Cost varies by sale price but typically ranges from $1,000 to $3,000 for properties in the $600,000-$1,000,000 range.

**Escrow Fees**: Usually split 50/50 between buyer and seller, though this is negotiable. Escrow fees typically range from $2 to $2.50 per $1,000 of sale price.

**Transfer Tax**: County transfer taxes in Los Angeles County are $1.10 per $1,000 of sale price. Some cities add additional transfer taxes.

**HOA Documentation Fees**: If your property is in an HOA (common in [Valencia](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/homes-for-sale-in-valencia-ca-over-1000000) and other planned communities), expect to pay $400-$800 for required HOA documents and transfer fees.

**Home Warranty**: If offering a home warranty to the buyer (common practice), expect $400-$600 for a one-year policy.

**Property Repairs**: Any repairs negotiated with the buyer or completed pre-sale to improve marketability.

**Staging Costs**: Optional but potentially valuable, ranging from $1,500-$5,000+ depending on scope.

**Example - Selling a $750,000 Home**:

	- Commission (5%): $37,500

	- Title Insurance: $2,200

	- Escrow Fees (half): $1,000

	- Transfer Tax: $825

	- HOA Documentation: $600

	- Home Warranty: $500

	- Misc. Costs: $500

	- **Total: Approximately $43,125 (5.75% of sale price)**

**Buyer Costs**

When buying property, buyers face these typical costs:

**Down Payment**: Typically 3-20% of purchase price depending on loan type. On a $750,000 home, this ranges from $22,500 (3%) to $150,000 (20%).

**Loan Origination/Lender Fees**: Typically 0.5-1% of loan amount, or $2,500-$5,000 on a $500,000 loan.

**Appraisal Fee**: Usually $500-$700 for single-family homes in Santa Clarita.

**Home Inspection**: Typically $400-$600 for a standard inspection, with additional costs for specialized inspections (roof, foundation, pool, etc.).

**Title Insurance (Lender's Policy)**: Buyers pay for the lender's title policy, typically $500-$1,500.

**Escrow Fees**: The buyer's half of escrow fees, typically $1,000-$2,000.

**Property Taxes**: Prorated property taxes from closing date through end of tax period.

**HOA Transfer Fees**: If applicable, typically $200-$500.

**Homeowners Insurance**: First year's premium paid at closing, typically $1,500-$3,000+ in California.

**Recording Fees**: County fees for recording the deed, typically $50-$200.

**Buyer's Agent Commission**: Only if you're paying directly rather than through seller concession.

**Example - Buying a $750,000 Home with 10% Down**:

	- Down Payment: $75,000

	- Loan Fees (1%): $6,750

	- Appraisal: $600

	- Inspection: $500

	- Title Insurance: $1,200

	- Escrow Fees (half): $1,000

	- Homeowners Insurance: $2,500

	- Property Taxes (prorated): $1,500

	- Recording Fees: $100

	- Misc. Costs: $500

	- **Total: Approximately $89,650 (11.95% of purchase price)**

**Note**: These costs assume the seller pays the buyer's agent commission through concession. If the buyer pays agent commission directly, add another 2-3% ($15,000-$22,500 on a $750,000 home).

**Strategies for Managing Costs**

Both buyers and sellers can employ strategies to manage transaction costs:

	- **Negotiate Who Pays What**: Many costs that are "customarily" paid by one party can actually be negotiated.

	- **Shop Lenders**: Loan fees and interest rates vary significantly between lenders. Compare multiple options.

	- **Bundle Services**: Some title/escrow companies offer package pricing.

	- **Request Seller Credits**: Buyers can request sellers to contribute to closing costs (common for [first-time buyers](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/buyers-guide)).

	- **Time Closing Strategically**: Closing at month's end reduces prepaid interest and prorated costs.

## How Can I Budget Properly for Buying or Selling a Home in Santa Clarita?

Proper budgeting is crucial for a smooth real estate transaction. Let's discuss how to approach this for both buyers and sellers.

**For Buyers: Beyond Your Down Payment**

Many first-time buyers make the mistake of saving for a down payment without accounting for all the other costs involved in purchasing a home. Here's how to budget comprehensively:

**Step 1: Calculate Your Complete Cash Requirement**

Using our earlier example of a $750,000 purchase:

	- Down Payment (10%): $75,000

	- Closing Costs (estimated 2-3%): $15,000-$22,500

	- **Total Cash Needed at Closing: $90,000-$97,500**

**Step 2: Add Moving and Transition Costs**

Beyond closing costs, budget for:

	- Moving expenses: $1,000-$5,000 depending on distance and whether you hire professionals

	- Immediate home repairs/improvements: $2,000-$10,000+

	- New furniture/appliances: Variable, but often $3,000-$10,000

	- Utility deposits and connection fees: $500-$1,000

	- HOA move-in fees (if applicable): $200-$500

**Step 3: Build an Emergency Reserve**

Always maintain liquid reserves after closing:

	- Minimum: 3-6 months of housing expenses (mortgage + insurance + taxes + HOA)

	- Recommended: 6-12 months for homeowners

	- On a $750,000 purchase, this might mean keeping $20,000-$40,000 in reserves

**Step 4: Account for Ongoing Costs**

Monthly homeownership costs include:

	- Mortgage principal + interest

	- Property taxes (approximately 1.25% annually in LA County, or $9,375/year on a $750,000 home)

	- Homeowners insurance ($2,500-$4,000+ annually)

	- HOA fees if applicable ($100-$500+ monthly depending on community)

	- Maintenance and repairs (estimate 1-2% of home value annually, or $7,500-$15,000/year)

	- Utilities (typically higher than rental properties due to size)

**For Sellers: Net Proceeds Calculation**

Sellers need to understand net proceeds‚Äîwhat you'll actually walk away with after all costs are paid.

**Calculating Net Proceeds**:

Sale Price: $750,000 Minus:

	- Existing Mortgage Balance: $400,000 (example)

	- Commissions (5%): $37,500

	- Title/Escrow/Fees: $5,000

	- Prorated Taxes: $1,500

	- Repairs/Credits to Buyer: $3,000

	- Payoff of Home Equity Line: $25,000 (if applicable) **Estimated Net Proceeds: $278,000**

**Important Considerations**:

	- **Capital Gains Taxes**: If you've lived in the home as your primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years, up to $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (married) of profit is tax-free. Consult a tax professional if your gain exceeds these amounts.

	- **Moving Costs**: Budget for moving, temporary housing if needed, and costs associated with your next home.

	- **Timing**: If you're buying another home, ensure closing dates align to avoid overlap periods where you're paying two mortgages or the need for temporary housing.

**Budget Planning Tools and Resources**

Several tools can help with budget planning:

	- **Mortgage Calculators**: Use online calculators to understand monthly payment impacts of different purchase prices and interest rates. Check out the [mortgage rates page](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/mortgage-rates) for current information.

	- **Net Sheet Calculators**: Many title companies provide estimated net sheets for sellers showing approximate proceeds.

	- **Lender Pre-Approval**: Getting pre-approved not only helps with home shopping but also provides clear numbers on your buying power and costs.

	- **Professional Consultation**: [Schedule a consultation](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/contact) to discuss your specific situation and get personalized guidance on budgeting for your transaction.

**Common Budgeting Mistakes to Avoid**

	- **Spending Your Entire Savings on Down Payment**: Always keep reserves for emergencies and unexpected costs

	- **Forgetting About Moving Costs**: These add up quickly

	- **Underestimating Ongoing Maintenance**: Homes require regular maintenance and occasional major repairs

	- **Not Accounting for Property Tax Increases**: Your property taxes will likely be reassessed after purchase based on the new purchase price

	- **Overlooking HOA Special Assessments**: If buying in an HOA community, review the HOA's financial health and ask about any planned special assessments

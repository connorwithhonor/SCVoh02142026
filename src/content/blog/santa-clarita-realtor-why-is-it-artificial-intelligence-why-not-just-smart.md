## The Dartmouth Conference: Where AI Was Born (And Why It Wasn't Called Something Cooler)

Let's rewind to 1956. Picture this: A bunch of eggheads gather at Dartmouth College for what's now hailed as the birthplace of AI as a formal field. John McCarthy, the guy who coined the term in 1955 (yep, he prepped it for the '56 conference), teams up with Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon. These weren't your average basement hackers; they were pioneers dreaming of machines that could mimic human smarts.

McCarthy defined AI as "the science and engineering of making intelligent machines." Simple, right? But why "artificial"? It stems from the Latin "artificialis," meaning "made by human skill" or "crafted." It's not about fakeness ‚Äì like those phony flavors in your diet soda ‚Äì but about deliberate creation. Humans building smarts from the ground up, contrasting with our squishy, biological brains.

Sarcasm alert: If only they'd called it "Man-Made Intelligence" (MMI), we could've avoided all the gender debates today. But nope, history shows no trace of MMI being floated. "Artificial" won for its precision and neutrality ‚Äì no ties to "man," and it sidestepped cybernetics, which was more about feedback loops than symbolic reasoning.

## AI's Etymology: From Latin Roots to Modern Misconceptions

Dig deeper into "artificial." Latin "ars" (art or skill) + "facere" (to make) = something crafted. It's the opposite of "naturalis," our innate smarts. No wonder experts gripe ‚Äì "artificial" evokes fake turf or sweeteners, not groundbreaking tech.

But here's the kicker: It's about artifice as in skillful creation. McCarthy chose it to highlight engineering over biology. Neutral, broad, and timeless. Alternatives like "synthetic intelligence" might fit better now, as AI generates its own training data, but tradition wins.

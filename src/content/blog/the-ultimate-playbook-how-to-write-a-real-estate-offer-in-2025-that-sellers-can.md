## The Pre-Game: Your Due Diligence Checklist Before You Dare Write an Offer

I know you're excited. But charging in with an offer before you've done your homework is like running into a building without knowing the layout. You‚Äôre going to get lost, and you‚Äôre probably going to get hurt. Yesterday, I covered the initial investigations, but they are so critical they bear repeating and expanding upon. These are the steps you take to arm yourself with intelligence.

### Interrogating the Homeowners Association (HOA)

If the property is in an HOA-controlled community‚Äîwhich many in Santa Clarita are‚Äîthe HOA is a gatekeeper you cannot ignore. Their rules, regulations (CC&amp;Rs), and financial health can dramatically impact your life and your wallet.

	- 
	**Pets Are People, Too (Sort Of):** Do you have three beloved pit bulls that you consider your children? Does your spouse collect exotic cats? You absolutely must verify the HOA's pet policy. Don't just ask if pets are allowed. Ask about **breed restrictions** (very common with dogs like Pit Bulls, Rottweilers, Dobermans), **weight limits**, and the **number of pets** allowed. I've seen deals fall apart because a buyer's furry family didn't meet the HOA's rigid criteria.

	

	- 
	**Trust, But Verify (And Get It in Writing):** Your agent says, "Don't worry, I'll check on it for you." That's nice. It's also not good enough. As someone who‚Äôs dealt with bureaucracy my whole life, I can tell you that the person your agent speaks to at the management company could be on their last day, hate their job, and give completely wrong information just for kicks. It happens. You, the buyer, need to make the call. And after you get a verbal confirmation, you follow up with a polite but firm email: *"Dear HOA Representative, Thank you for your time today. To confirm our conversation, you stated that my three pit bulls, weighing approximately 60 pounds each, are permitted within the community. Please reply to confirm this is correct. Thank you."* That email is your evidence. It's your protection. Without it, their verbal "sure, no problem" is worthless.

	

### Beyond the Four Walls: Neighborhood Reconnaissance

You're not just buying a structure of wood and drywall; you're buying a location. You're buying neighbors, traffic patterns, and a lifestyle.

	- 
	**Become a Detective:** Drive the neighborhood at different times. What's it like at 7 AM on a Monday? How about 9 PM on a Friday? Are there cars parked everywhere? Do you hear non-stop barking dogs? Do the neighbors' yards look like a junkyard? These are clues about your future quality of life. Use tools like Google Maps Street View to get a preliminary look, but nothing beats putting boots on the ground.

	

	- 
	**Check the Official Records:** Visit the local police department's website or use online crime mapping tools. Are you moving into a hotspot for petty theft or noise complaints? This is public information. While you're at it, check the sex offender registry for the area. These are uncomfortable topics, but being uncomfortable for ten minutes during your investigation is infinitely better than being miserable for ten years in the wrong house.

	

Completing this level of due diligence *before* you write the offer does two things. First, it ensures you actually want the house and its accompanying lifestyle. Second, it gives you leverage. If you discover issues, they can become negotiating points. But most importantly, it allows you to write your offer from a position of confidence, not hopeful ignorance.

## The Art of the Offer Price: A Science, Not a Guessing Game

This is where the rubber meets the road. The price you offer is the single most important number in the entire transaction, and too many buyers pull it out of thin air. They base it on the Zestimate (a notoriously flawed algorithm), what they *feel* the house is worth, or what their Uncle Bob who bought a house in 1982 thinks. This is financial suicide. A winning offer price is the result of a cold, hard, data-driven analysis.

### Look Backward to Move Forward: Why Sold Comps are Your Bible

Listen carefully: **The seller's asking price is a fantasy.** It‚Äôs their wish list. It‚Äôs the number they hope some uniformed buyer will pay. Active listings from other sellers are also fantasies. The only numbers that matter are the closing prices of **sold comparable properties** (comps) within the last 180 days. This is what an appraiser will use, so it‚Äôs what you must use.

Your agent should pull a Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) for you. But you need to understand how to read it.

	- 
	**The 180-Day Rule:** Focus on homes sold in the last six months. Anything older is stale data from a different market. In a rapidly changing market, you might even narrow that to 90 days.

	

	- 
	**Proximity is Key:** The comps should be as close to your target property as possible. Ideally in the same tract or neighborhood. Going a mile away introduces too many variables.

	

	- 
	**The Magic Number is Three:** An appraiser needs at least three solid, recent, and nearby comps to establish value. Your pricing strategy should be built on this foundation.

	

### Apples to Apples: Matching Property Specifications

Not all four-bedroom houses are created equal. You have to dissect the comps and adjust for differences.

	- 
	**Square Footage:** This is the big one. The comps should be within 10-15% of the square footage of your target house. In Santa Clarita, with its many tract developments from builders like Lennar and D.R. Horton, it's often possible to find comps with the *exact same square footage*. These are gold.

	

	- 
	**Bedrooms &amp; Bathrooms:** You must compare a 4-bed, 2.5-bath house to other 4-bed, 2.5-bath houses. Understand the difference:

	
		
		**Half Bath:** Toilet and sink.

		

		- 
		**Three-Quarter Bath:** Toilet, sink, and a shower (no tub).

		

		- 
		**Full Bath:** Toilet, sink, and a tub (or a separate tub and shower).

		

	

	
	- 
	**Condition, Upgrades, and Lot:** This is where the "art" part comes in. You and your agent need to make value adjustments.

	
		
		Does the comp have a brand-new kitchen while your target house has original 1990s oak cabinets? You offer less.

		

		- 
		Does your target house have a premium lot with a stunning view while the comp backs up to a busy street? Your target house is worth more.

		

		- 
		New roof? Pool? A three-car garage versus a two-car? All of these factors require a dollar-value adjustment when comparing properties.

		

	

	

### Strategic Offers: Lowballing vs. Justified Pricing

Armed with your data, you can now build a strategy.

	- 
	**The "Cheeky" Offer:** Your buyer might say, "Let's just throw in a low offer and see what happens!" As an agent, I will write any legal offer my client wants. But I will also explain the likely outcome. In a competitive situation, an unjustified low offer is an insult and will likely be ignored. However, if a house has been sitting on the market for 60 days with multiple price drops, a well-timed, slightly lower offer might just work. The key is understanding the context.

	

	- 
	**The Data-Backed Offer:** This is the professional approach. When I submit an offer below asking price for a client, it's not a random number. It's accompanied by a mini-CMA showing the seller and their agent *exactly* how we arrived at that price. "Mr. and Mrs. Seller, we are offering $X. As you can see from the three attached comparable sales, all of which closed in the last 90 days within a quarter-mile of your home, the adjusted market value is $Y. Our offer reflects this data." This transforms you from a bargain hunter into an educated, serious buyer. It forces them to negotiate on facts, not emotion.

	

	- 
	**Offering Above Asking:** In a hot market, you may need to go above the list price. The same rules apply. You analyze the comps to see if other homes are selling for over ask. If so, by how much? Is there a trend of sales prices being 3-5% over list? Your offer should reflect that reality.

	

Your offer price is your opening statement in a high-stakes negotiation. Make sure it‚Äôs intelligent, justified, and strategic. ## Beyond the Price Tag: Crafting Terms That Tip the Scales

Sometimes, the winning offer isn't the highest one. It's the one with the strongest, cleanest, and most seller-friendly terms. Price gets you in the game, but terms can win it for you.

### Show Me the Money: Your Financing is Your Calling Card

The seller isn't just selling a house; they are trying to mitigate risk. Your financing is the biggest risk factor for them. A deal that falls apart because the buyer's loan gets denied is a seller's worst nightmare.

	- 
	**Loan Type Matters:**

	
		
		**Cash is King:** Obviously. If you're a cash buyer, you're at the top of the food chain.

		

		- 
		**Conventional Loan:** Generally viewed as the strongest financing type. It often requires a larger down payment and has stricter underwriting standards, which gives sellers more confidence.

		

		- 
		**FHA/VA Loans:** These are fantastic programs that enable many people to buy homes. However, some sellers view them as slightly riskier due to more flexible underwriting and potentially stricter appraisal requirements. It's not always fair, but it's the reality of perception.

		

	

	
	- 
	**Down Payment:** A larger down payment signals financial strength. An offer with 20% down looks more solid to a seller than one with 3% down, even if both buyers are pre-approved.

	

	- 
	**The Pre-Approval Letter:** A standard pre-approval is the bare minimum. A **Direct Underwriter (DU) Approval** is far more powerful. This means an actual underwriter has reviewed your file, not just a loan officer running numbers through software. It‚Äôs as close to a guaranteed loan as you can get. If you want to get pre-approved the right way, start here: [https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/get-pre-approved](https://www.santaclaritaopenhouses.com/get-pre-approved).

	

### Time is Money: The Power of Compressed Timelines

A standard California purchase agreement gives the buyer 17 days to complete their inspections and investigations. Shortening this timeline can be a huge carrot for the seller.

	- 
	**The Inspection Contingency:** Offering to do your inspections in 7 or 10 days instead of 17 shows the seller you are serious, organized, and mean business. It tells them you won't be dragging your feet and that they'll know quickly if the deal is solid.

	

	- 
	**A Word of Warning:** Do NOT do what buyers were doing in the crazy market of a few years ago: waiving contingencies entirely. Waiving your right to inspect, your appraisal contingency, or your loan contingency is playing Russian roulette with your financial future. Buyers were forfeiting their 3% deposit ($30,000 on a $1M home) an hour after acceptance because they got cold feet. They were paying tens of thousands in cash over a low appraisal. This is reckless. Shorten your timeframes, yes. Waive your fundamental protections? Absolutely not.

	

	- 
	**The Seller Rent-Back:** This is a simple but effective term. Offer to let the sellers stay in the house for a few days (or even longer, structured as a formal rental) after the escrow closes. This relieves their stress of having to move out immediately and can be an incredibly valuable, non-monetary concession that makes your offer stand out.

	

## The Presentation: The Final, Critical Step to Victory

You can have the best price and the best terms, but if you present it poorly, you can still lose. The offer submission is your closing argument.

### The Complete Package

Your agent shouldn't just email a one-page offer. They should submit a professional package that includes:

	- 
	**The Purchase Agreement:** Perfectly filled out, no mistakes.

	

	- 
	**Proof of Funds:** Bank statements (with account numbers blacked out) showing you have the cash for the down payment and closing costs.

	

	- 
	**The Lender Letter:** A strong DU approval letter is best.

	

	- 
	**The Justification:** If offering under asking, include the data-backed CMA.

	

	- 
	**A Cover Letter/Email:** A brief, professional summary of why your offer is the strongest. This is where your agent can mention the due diligence you've done, which shows your commitment. (Note: Personal "love letters" from the buyer are now discouraged due to fair housing concerns, but the agent can still convey your seriousness professionally).

	

### The Secret Weapon: The Lender-to-Agent Phone Call

This is it. This is the one thing that 99% of buyers' agents and lenders fail to do, and it is the most powerful tool in your arsenal.

Once your offer is submitted, **your mortgage lender needs to personally call the listing agent.**

Think about it from the listing agent's perspective. They have five offers sitting in their inbox. They're all pretty similar. Then their phone rings. It's the lender for "Buyer A." The lender says, *"Hi, this is [Lender's Name]. I'm calling about the offer from the Smith family. I just wanted to personally let you know that I have taken them through full underwriting. Their income, assets, and credit are rock solid. They are as good as gold, and we are ready to close in 21 days. If you have any questions at all about their financial strength, please call me directly."*

What does this do? In that one-minute phone call, you have just vaulted your offer to the top of the pile. The listing agent can now go to their seller and say, "We have five offers, but only one of them had a lender who was professional and proactive enough to call me and personally vouch for them. These are the serious players."

It‚Äôs shocking how rarely this happens. It's the difference between being passive and being proactive. It shows you have a professional, dedicated team behind you. Insist that your agent and your lender execute this play. It costs nothing, and it can win you the house.
